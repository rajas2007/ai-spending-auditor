# USER_INTERVIEWS.md

# Interview 1

## Person
Soham Kale — Senior friend working at a newly built startup.

## Company Stage
Early-stage startup.

---

## Key Quotes

> “Our AI subscriptions started casually but now they’re becoming an actual operational expense.”

> “Different people in the team prefer different AI tools, so we ended up paying for overlapping products.”

> “A tool like this is useful because startups usually don’t notice these costs until they scale.”

> “I would probably share this internally during budgeting discussions because it’s easier than manually explaining the overlap.”

---

## Most Surprising Insight

The most surprising thing was that even small startups already experience AI tool sprawl very early. I originally assumed only larger teams would have overlapping subscriptions and unclear spending patterns.

---

## What It Changed About My Design

This conversation made me focus more on:
- overlap detection
- shareable public reports
- clear savings presentation
- simple recommendations instead of overly technical analytics

It reinforced that the product should feel lightweight and easy to discuss internally.

# Interview 2

## Person
Shreya Tiwari — Senior currently interning at an MNC.

## Company Stage
Large enterprise/MNC environment.

---

## Key Quotes

> “In bigger organizations, people often don’t even know which AI tools are officially approved versus personally subscribed.”

> “A lot of teams keep paying for tools simply because nobody takes ownership of reviewing them.”

> “The most important thing is whether the recommendations feel financially reasonable.”

> “If I shared this report with someone in management, it should look professional and trustworthy.”

---

## Most Surprising Insight

The most surprising insight was that even large organizations often lack centralized visibility into AI tooling spend. I initially thought larger companies would already have mature internal systems for this.

---

## What It Changed About My Design

After this discussion, I improved:
- report presentation quality
- readability
- professional-looking summaries
- clean PDF exports

It also influenced my decision to keep the audit reasoning deterministic instead of overly AI-generated so the recommendations would feel more trustworthy.

# Interview 3

## Person
Anay Bhosle — Senior one year ahead of me with experience in multiple college, state-level, and national-level competitions and hackathons.

## Company Stage
Student builder / hackathon ecosystem.

---

## Key Quotes

> “Students and indie builders subscribe to AI tools impulsively when they see productivity hype online.”

> “Most people never reevaluate whether they still need all the subscriptions they started with.”

> “The public report sharing is probably the strongest feature because people like comparing stacks and recommendations.”

> “This feels more like a startup utility than a college project.”

---

## Most Surprising Insight

The most surprising thing was realizing that even students and hackathon participants are already building surprisingly complex AI stacks with multiple subscriptions across coding, research, and content workflows.

---

## What It Changed About My Design

This conversation pushed me to improve:
- onboarding simplicity
- faster audit generation
- visual polish
- screenshot-friendly layouts
- social/shareable aspects of the report

It also changed how I thought about virality and product positioning because users were naturally interested in comparing and discussing their AI stacks.
