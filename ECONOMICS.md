# Unit Economics Analysis

If Credex deployed Aethra publicly, the economics would primarily depend on whether the tool could consistently identify startups with meaningful AI overspend and convert a portion of those users into infrastructure credit customers.

The average value of a converted lead to Credex could reasonably be estimated between $1,000–$5,000 annually depending on the size of the startup and the scale of AI infrastructure usage. Early-stage startups may only purchase a few hundred dollars of discounted credits initially, while larger engineering-heavy teams could represent significantly higher-value recurring accounts.

For rough modeling, I assumed:
- average successful customer value: $2,500/year
- gross margin contribution to Credex: ~20–30%
- meaningful conversion opportunities primarily come from teams already spending heavily on AI tooling

The most important metric is not total visitors, but qualified audits from teams already deeply using AI products.

For customer acquisition cost (CAC), the earliest channels would likely remain extremely low-cost because distribution is highly community-driven and content-driven.

Estimated early-stage CAC assumptions:

| Channel | Estimated CAC |
|---|---|
| X/Twitter organic posts | $5–15 |
| Reddit/community engagement | $10–20 |
| Hacker News/Product Hunt exposure | $20–40 |
| Founder referrals/shared reports | <$10 |

Because the product itself generates shareable reports, distribution partially compounds through users discussing and comparing AI tooling stacks publicly.

The core conversion funnel might realistically look like this:

| Funnel Stage | Example Conversion |
|---|---|
| Landing Page Visitor → Audit Generated | 20–30% |
| Audit Generated → Email Capture | 35–50% |
| Email Capture → Consultation Request | 10–15% |
| Consultation → Paid Credit Purchase | 20–30% |

Using rough numbers:

10,000 visitors/month

→ 2,500 completed audits  
→ 1,000 captured leads  
→ 120 consultation calls  
→ 30 paying customers

If each converted customer generates approximately $2,500 annually, that becomes:

30 × $2,500 = $75,000 ARR added per month cohort

Over time, even modest retention and recurring usage could compound significantly.

For the tool to contribute toward $1M ARR within roughly 18 months, several things would need to become true simultaneously:

1. The audit reports must become naturally shareable among founders and engineering teams.

2. The product must consistently surface meaningful savings opportunities rather than generic recommendations.

3. Credex would need strong operational follow-up after lead capture to convert high-savings users into paying infrastructure customers.

4. The viral/shareable loop through public reports would need to reduce paid acquisition dependency.

A simplified target path to $1M ARR might look like:

- 15,000–20,000 monthly visitors
- 4,000–5,000 completed audits
- 400–600 high-intent leads monthly
- 20–40 monthly converted customers
- average customer value around $2,000–$3,000 annually

This is aggressive but not unrealistic if:
- AI tooling adoption continues accelerating
- operational AI spend becomes more visible
- startups increasingly seek optimization rather than unrestricted experimentation

The strongest aspect of the economics is that the product itself acts as both:
- a useful standalone utility
- a lead qualification system

Users generating large projected savings are naturally the most valuable leads for Credex. That alignment significantly improves the efficiency of the funnel compared to generic outbound acquisition.

The biggest risk is that many users may only use the tool occasionally rather than repeatedly. Because of that, the product’s success depends more on:
- shareability
- trustworthiness
- strong lead qualification
- operational usefulness

than traditional SaaS retention metrics alone.
